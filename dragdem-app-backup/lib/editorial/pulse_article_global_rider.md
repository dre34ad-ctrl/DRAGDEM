# Industry Insights | The Global Rider: Technical Specs for Touring

## The Art of the International Stage: Beyond the Glitter

You’ve booked the flight. You’ve secured the visa. Your costumes are vacuum-sealed and your tracks are mastered. But as any seasoned international performer knows, the most dangerous part of a global tour isn't the customs line—it’s the stage you haven’t seen yet.

In the world of professional drag, "technical difficulties" is often code for "unprepared venues." When you transition from the high-production warehouses of Brooklyn to the intimate, high-energy corridors of Bangkok or the experimental undergrounds of Berlin, the technical landscape shifts beneath your heels. To thrive globally, you need more than talent; you need **Technical Normalization.**

## 1. Regional Realities: BKK vs. Berlin vs. NYC

Every global drag hub has a distinct "technical DNA." Understanding these differences is the first step toward a flawless set.

### **Bangkok: High-Intensity Integration**
In hubs like **Silom (Stranger Bar, Maggie Choo’s)**, stages are often designed for high-impact, close-proximity performance. While the square footage might be tighter than a US theater, the lighting and sound integration is often incredibly dense. 
*   **The Nuance:** Expect fast-paced cues and a high reliance on wireless mic stability. Your rider needs to specify "Minimum Ceiling Height" for stunts and "Quick-Change Proximity."

### **Berlin: The Alternative Standard**
Berlin’s scene, from **SchwuZ** to **Tipsy Bear**, prides itself on alternative and political storytelling. This often means performing in non-traditional spaces—converted warehouses or historic cabaret basements.
*   **The Nuance:** Staging surfaces vary wildly. A rider that doesn't specify "Wood or Marley floors only" might find you performing on industrial concrete. Berlin seekers value the "Technical Handshake"—clear, concise audio-visual requirements that respect the venue's unique architecture.

### **New York City: The Cabaret Legacy**
NYC is a city of extremes. You might find yourself on the massive, high-production stage of **3 Dollar Bill** one night and a historic, cramped cabaret riser in the West Village the next.
*   **The Nuance:** Power distribution and "Backstage Footprint" are critical. NYC venues are often space-constrained; your rider must define exactly how much room your costumes and entourage require to avoid "Backstage Friction."
